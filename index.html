Understanding Our Thinking System

How We Evaluate Coherence, Plausibility & Probability

Kahneman's Framework
Our minds operate through two systems: System 1 (fast, intuitive, automatic) and System 2 (slow, deliberate, effortful). Understanding how these systems evaluate coherence, plausibility, and probability is key to understanding human reasoning.

System 1 Dominant

Definition: How well ideas fit together and make sense as a unified whole.

System 1 in Action: When you hear "banker, conservative, plays golf," your mind instantly creates a coherent story. System 1 loves patterns and will create coherence even from limited information.

How Coherence Works:

1
System 1 rapidly connects available information
2
Creates a "good story" that feels complete
3
Ignores missing information (WYSIATI - What You See Is All There Is)
üéØ Plausibility
System 1 Primary
System 2 Secondary

Definition: How believable or reasonable something seems based on our existing knowledge and experience.

Click to explore...

Availability Heuristic: We judge plausibility by how easily we can recall similar examples. Recent plane crashes make flying seem less plausible as "safe," even though statistics show otherwise.

Plausibility Assessment:

1
System 1 checks against familiar patterns and experiences
2
Uses representativeness heuristic (does this match our stereotype?)
3
System 2 may override with deliberate analysis (if engaged)

System 2 Dominant

Definition: The mathematical likelihood that something will occur, based on statistical reasoning.

The Conjunction Fallacy: "Linda is a bank teller and feminist" seems more probable than "Linda is a bank teller" because it's more coherent with our mental model‚Äîbut it's statistically impossible.

Probability Reasoning:

1
Requires System 2's deliberate, statistical thinking
2
Often overridden by System 1's coherence and plausibility
3
Needs conscious effort to apply correctly

üéØ Key Takeaway

System 1 prioritizes coherence and plausibility over actual probability. This creates compelling narratives but can lead to systematic errors in judgment. Effective thinking requires knowing when to engage System 2's more careful statistical reasoning.

The Hierarchy in Our Minds:
1. Coherence (Does it make a good story?)
2. Plausibility (Does it feel believable?)
3. Probability (What do the numbers actually say?)
üîç Practice Scenarios

Medical Diagnosis

Situation: A patient has a rare symptom. Two explanations: common cold (boring but statistically likely) vs. rare tropical disease (exciting and fits the unusual symptom perfectly).

System 1: Favors the rare disease (high coherence with unusual symptom)

System 2: Considers base rates‚Äîcommon conditions are common

Lesson: "When you hear hoofbeats, think horses, not zebras"

Business Decision

Situation: Choosing between a well-researched but boring strategy vs. an innovative approach with a compelling story.

System 1: Attracted to the coherent innovation narrative

System 2: Weighs actual success probabilities and risk factors

Lesson: Great stories don't always make great strategies

Personal Judgment

Situation: Meeting someone new who fits perfectly into a stereotype you hold.

System 1: Creates coherent impression based on limited cues

System 2: Considers individual differences and witholds judgment

Lesson: First impressions are coherent but not necessarily accurate
